The Invention of the Internet

Before we had super computers that could talk to one another from across the world, we had ineffective hardware that would take up entire rooms or buildings. These computers were simple machines that had no appeal to the public and were only capable of crunching numbers. With the launch of Sputnik however, the United States got a wake up call and decided to advance technology to allow man walk on the moon. This back and forth race was like the modern Industrial Revolution and innovated many different areas of tech such as:

  • Wireless Headsets
  • Cordless Vacuums
  • Laptops
  • And Much More!

The focus changes to ARPA, the Advanced Research Project Agency, which tackled the idea of allowing two computers to exchange information. This initiative was led by Robert Taylor who thought of having separate, smaller computers that connected university terminals. Since they were the first to attempt to create such a network, they had to consider many roadblocks like:

  • What would happen if too much data was sent?
  • How would the sender know if the destination got their packages?
  • What if the computers aren't communicating in the same language?

Fortunately ARPA overcame these obstacles and had four connected computers in universities across the U.S. by 1969. This number rapidly expanded and most universities would find themselves using these communicating computers. The popularity of these computers exploded with the creation of email in 1971 which brought a new audience: companies.


The internet would become very accessible by the public with two monumental events: the HPCA (High Performance Computing Act) of 1991 and the browser war. The HPCA released the internet from the government into the hands of the public. This act was quickly followed by the browser war where companies would work endlessly to increase the ease of access of the internet with better graphical interfaces.
